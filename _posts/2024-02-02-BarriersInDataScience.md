Data science is a hot career right now. Organizations with massive amounts of data need people who can glean insights from it. Artificial intelligence and machine learning are booming. 

But like many careers in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM), data science is not very accessible to people with disabilties. [This presentation by Dr. Andrew Hasley](https://youtu.be/qmQWED1PWKc?si=eEAYnJoX1_fRTPY-) discusses observations of the barriers in the data science workforce pipeline. In this post, I will further explore accessibility and data science at the micro-, meso-, and macro- levels.

# The Micro- Level

## Individual Accessibility Needs

Each disabled person has their own unique profile of access needs. Meeting access needs is a must for the success of people with disabilities in data science. This may include access to informal and formal disability accommodations. 

# The Meso- Level

## Access to Disability Accommodations

In both education and the workplace, disability accommodations are an important tool for the success of disabled people. However, formal accommodations processess can have many barriers at each step of the process. An intersectional approach (i.e. one that considers how a person's social and political identities result in privilege or discrimination in different situations) is required to best understand the problem with currently existing accommodations processes. 

The first step of the formal accommodations process typically involves evaluation for eligibility. This often requires medical documentation. This is a barrier for people who are low-income, have precarious immigration status, and other populations historically oppressed by the biomedical establishment (e.g. people of color and the LGBTQ+ community). 

After eligibility is determined and accommodations are agreed upon with the educational institution or employer, they must be implemented and enforced. However, implementation and enforcement does not always go as smoothly as expected. Given the power dynamic that exists between student/professor or employee/employer, it can be difficult to hold professors or employers accountable for not meeting accommodations. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) also has been criticized for its issues in enforcement. Additionally, current systems of accommodations expect an overreliance on self-advocacy of the disabled person. A disabled person is expected to be on top of all their documentation, continually advocate for their needs, and manage any issues that arise. That can be exhausting work!

For more information about accommodations in the workplace, visit [The Job Accommodation Network](https://askjan.org/).

## Inaccessible Data Science Tools

It's no secret that data science requires coding, statistical information, and data visualization. However, tools we use are not always made with accessibility in mind. Given the open-source nature of some of our tools, it is even more difficult and inconsistent to find accessibility practices. Additionally, in our education the practices we are taught are not always the most accessible either (e.g. the best accessibility practices for data visualization).


# The Macro- Level

## The Leaky Pipeline

In general, there is a leaky pipeline for disabled people pursuing careers in STEM. 

> "Leaky pipeline is often used as a metaphorical reference to the way that members of certain Demographics fail to continue progression towards particular careers, leading to underrepresentation in related industries." [^1] 

In "Academic pipeline for scientists with disabilities," the authors Booksh and Madsen mention the existence of the leaky pipeline in STEM degree attainment for disabled students in both undergraduate and graduate programs. [^2] For more information on diversity in STEM, visit [this report](https://ncses.nsf.gov/pubs/nsf23315/report) from the National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics. Additionally, the National Academies of Sciences are working on a publication related to this topic as well.[^3]

## Ableism

The pervasive structural issue of ableism remains a significant barrier to all disabled people. Most simply, ableism is the privileging of nondisabled bodyminds over disabled bodyminds. Ableism affects how we think about and interact with disabled people, including expectations and stereotypes. Ableism is the force behind stigma and violence against disabled people. When exploring the barriers to people with disabilties in data science, it must be a multifactorial analysis that includes a good understanding of ableism.
